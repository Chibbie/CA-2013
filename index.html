        • enables 3rd parties to know what powers/activities company can engage in
        • public doc, everyone person involved presumed to have knowledge of it
        • subs must know purposes for which their money will be used and what risks

    • Features of company
      • Separate legal identity
        • Definition
          • clothed with legal personality
          • existence distinct and separate from its members
          • can own property, have bank account, raise loans, incur liabilities and enter into contracts.
          • Even members can contract with company and will be treated like outsiders
        • Case Law
          • "A member does not even have insurable interest in the property of the company
            • Macaura vs Northern Assurance Co Limited. (1925)
              • Macaura holder of 99% shares of a timber company
              • Major creditor of the company, M insured company's timber in his own name
              • Timber lost in fire, M claimed insurance compensation.
              • Held, the insurance company not liable to him as no shareholder has any right to any item of property owned by the company, for he has no legal or equitable interest in them
      • Perpetual Succession
        • Members may change or die, company goes on till wound up on grounds specified by this Act.
        • Law alone can bring end to its life, existence not affected by death or insolvency of its members, or shares changing hands indefinitely
      • Limited Liability
        • Limited Liability Company
          • Debts of company not debts of its members.
          • Members only liable upto the nominal value of shares held by them
        • Company limited by guarantee
          • liable only to the extent of amount guaranteed by them, that too when company goes into liquidation
        • Unlimited company
          • liability of its members is unlimited as well
      • Artificial Legal Person
        • created by a process other than natural birth
        • It is a person since clothed with all rights of an individual
        • It can own property and stuff, sue get sued.
        • Company is artificial person, it can act only through some human agency, viz. directors
          • Directions can control affairs of the company and act as its agency but not agents of the members of the company.
          • Directors can either on their own or through common seal, authenticate its formal acts
      • Common Seal
        • official signature
        • affixed by the officers and employees of the company on its every document.
        • used as symbol of incorporation
        • Companies Amendment act 2015, made seal optional.
          • because seal is seen as relic of medieval times
        • If no seal then authorization shall be made by two directors or by a director and a CS.
    • Corporate Veil Theory
      • Definition
        • refers to legal concept whereby company is seperate from its members
        • Members shielded from liabilites arising from companies acts
        • Enjoy corporate insulation
      • Case Law
        • Salomon vs Salomon and Co Ltd.
          • Facts
            • He incorporated a company named as such
            • with seven subscribers, him and his wife + 4 sons and 1 daughter
            • took over personal assets of Salomon and Salomon got 20000 shares and debentures worth 10000 EU
            • with charge over company's assets and balance in cash
            • company went into liquidation due to general trade depression
            • unsecured creditors to the tune of 7000 EU contended that
              • Salomon could not be treated as a secured creditor in respect of debentures held by him
              • as he was MD of 1 man company
              • the cloak of the company was mere sham and fraud
          • Verdict
            • Lord Mac Naughten
              • The company at law is a different person altogether from
              • the subscribers to the memorandum
              • and though it may be that after incorporation the business is precisely the same as before
              • and same persons are managers
              • and same hands receive the profits,
              • the company is not in law the agent of the subscribers or trustees for them.
      • Lifting it
        • Definition
          • looking behind the company as a legal person
          • disregarding the corporate entity
          • pay regard to the realities behind the legal facade
          • when Courts concern themselves directly with the members or managers
          • the corporate veil is said to be lifted
          • This is done when questions of control are involved rather than merely a question of ownership
        • When?

          • To determine the character of company i.e. to find out whether enemy or friend

            • Case Law
              • Daimler Co. Ltd vs Continental Tyre and Rubber co
                • Court may rend the veil for ascertaining whether a company is an enemy company
                • be characterised as an enemy company if affairs are in control of people of an enemy country
          • To protect revenue/tax
            • Where the company is used to evade or circumvent tax
            • Case Law
              • Dinshaw Maneckjee Petit
                • he earned huge incomes by way of dividends and interest.
                • he opened some companies and purchased their shares in exchange of his income by way of dividend and interest
                • Income was transferred back to him by way of loan.
                • Court decided that the private companies were a sham and corporate veil was lifted to decide the real owner of the incomes
          • To avoid a legal obligation
            • Case Law: Workmen of Associated Rubber Industry Ltd Vs Associated Rubber Industry Ltd.
              • Facts
                • Dividends from A company were coming in and used to pay bonus to workers
                • Company transferred these to a subsidiary wholly owned by it
                • Which meant these dividends were no longer used to calculate bonus
              • Verdict
                • SC brushed aside existence of subsidiary
                • it was serving no purpose except to reduce the gross profit of principal company in a bid to reduce its liability to pay bonus
          • Formation of subsidiaries to act as agents
            • Company sometimes regarded as an agent of some person, members or another companies
            • It is said to have lost its individuality in favour or its principal
            • Case Law:
              • Merchandise Transport Limited VS British Transport Commission (1982)
                • Company wanted to obtain license but couldn't do so if it applied in its own name
                • made a subsidiary and applied through it, with intention of transferring ownership of vehicles to subsidiary so formed.
                • Held, the both were on commercial unit and application was rejected
          • Company formed for fraud/improper conduct or to defeat law
            • To avoid legal obligations or to defeat or circumvent law, defraud creditors or avoid legal obligations,
              • Gilford Motor vs Horne
    • Types of company

    • Doctrine of ultra vires
      • Meaning
        • "Beyond (their) powers"
          • "Ultra vires the company
          • Ultravires contract can never be made binding on company
          • can sue on it or be sued on it
        • Legal act outside of Object clause is void and null
        • ultravires can't be ratified by unanimous consent of all members
        • Act which ultravires the directors but intravires the company can be ratified by a special resolution
        • If act ultravires the Articles, can be ratified by altering the Articles by a Special Resolution at a general meeting
        • Its purpose is now defeated as Object clause can easily be altered
          • by Special Resolution
      • Case Law
        • Ashbury Railway Carriage and Iron Company Limited V. Riche (1875)
          • Facts
            • Main objects of company
              • make, sell or lend on hire, railway carriages and wagons
              • carry on business of mechanical engineers and general contractors
              • purchase, lease, sell and work mines
              • purchase and sell as merchants or agents, coal, timber, metals, etc.
            • The directors entered into a contract with Riche
            • To finance construction of a railway line in Belgium
            • Shareholders passed special resolution to ratify act of directors
            • but they repudiated this contract as being ultravires
          • Contention of Riche
            • That contract was within meaning of general contractors
            • ratified by majority of shareholders
          • Verdict
            • held that contract was null and void
            • term general contracts was associated with mechanical engineers
            • had to be read in connection with company's main business
            • if the term general contractor's was not so interpreted
              • it would authorize the making of contracts of any kind and every description , ex marine and fire insurance

    • Doctrine of Indoor Management
      • Doctrine of Constructive Notice
        • Section 399, states that every document is available publicly
        • It is presumed that any person dealing with company have knowledge of
          • AoA, MoA or any Special Resolution(registrable) passed by company
        • such presumed/implied notice is called Constructive notice
      • Doctrine of Indoor Management
        • exception to Constructive Notice
        • outsider is entitled to assume that all the detailed formalities for doing some act have been observed
        • So as long the act is under AoA if done in a particular manner then outsider assumes that it 'll be done in a particular manner
        • Assume that everything is done according to procedure laid down in the Articles
        • Called the "Turquand Rule"
      • Landmark Case Law: The Royal British Bank vs. Turquand
        • Facts
          • Mr.Turquand was official liquidator of a company
          • Company had a bond for 2000EU to the RBB
          • which secured drawings on its current account
          • Bond was under company's seal, signed by two directors and the secretary
          • Company was sued
          • It was alleged that under AoA, directors power was to an amount authorized by a company resolution
          • A resolution had been passed but without specifying how much the directors could borrow
        • Verdict
          • that the bond was valid, RBB could enforce the terms.
          • bank was deemed to be aware that directors could borrow only to the amount resolutions allowed
          • AoA was registered so Constructive notice was there
          • But bank could not be deemed to know which ordinary resolutions passed, because they were not registrable
          • there was no requirement to look into company's internal workings.
          • "THE COMPANY'S INDOOR AFFAIRS ARE THE COMPANY"S PROBLEMS"
      • Exception to the doctrine of Indoor management
        • Actual or constructive knowledge of irregularity
          • Does not protect any person who has those
          • Case Law
            • Howard vs Patent Ivory Manufacturing Co.
              • Directors issued debentures to themselves to lend money to company
              • but the assent of shareholders was requirement to lend that big a amount
              • They had that knowledge so they couldn't plea turquand rule
        • Suspicion of Irregularity
          • Rule doesn't reward those who behave negligently
          • Should do inquiry where it invites inquiry
          • Case Law:
            • Anand Bihari Lal vs Dinshaw and Co
              • plaintiff accepted transfer of property
              • from accountant of some company
              • Held he should've known that an accountant
              • without the power of attorney had no authority to do so
        • Forgery
          • applies to irregularities which might otherwise affect a transaction but not to forgery
          • Case Law
            • Ruben v Great Fingall Consolidated
              • Plaintiff was transferee of a share certificate under the seal of defendant's company. The company's secretary forged the signature of two directors and issued the certificate
              • Held that, the rule has never been extended to cover such a complete forgery.
